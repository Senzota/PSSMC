Research relevance

Why this matters

Chatter vibration in CNC turning quietly limits how far a process can be pushed — it spoils surface finish, shortens tool life, and forces conservative parameter choices that reduce throughput. The problem is especially persistent because it is self‑excited: each new cut is shaped by the waviness left by the previous one, and small disturbances can grow rapidly into destructive oscillations.

This project traces a single study that starts with measured cutting‑force and vibration data, builds a power‑law force model, and lifts that static relation into a dynamic plant that a predictive sliding mode controller can shape. Every step is documented as it is completed, so the reasoning behind each modelling choice is transparent and easy to follow.

About this project

This archive documents the complete research pipeline for designing a Predictive Smoothed Sliding Mode Controller (PSSMC) that suppresses regenerative chatter in the turning of AISI 1045 steel by dynamically adjusting depth of cut in real time. Hoping to extend this novelty throughout all materials in turning operations.

The study is carried out by His Majesty Shuma Fadhil (Reg: 2024‑06‑02219), with his sensei Dr. Harryson at the University of Dar es Salaam, Department of Mechanical and Industrial Engineering. Each stage is documented as it is completed, making this site a live companion to the research rather than a retrospective summary.

The project is ongoing, so check back regularly for updates and new content as the study progresses through modelling, controller design, and evaluation.

Chatter vibration

Chatter is a self‑excited machining vibration driven by a regenerative mechanism: the tool cuts a surface that already carries waviness from the previous pass. If the phase and amplitude conditions are right, each cut amplifies the waviness left by the last one, and the oscillation grows until the tool loses contact or the material behaves nonlinearly.

In practice, chatter is heard as a loud, harsh tone and is visible as a regular ripple pattern on the finished surface. Suppressing it means either changing the operating point or actively adjusting a process parameter — in this study, depth of cut — to steer the system away from the unstable regime.

Empirical power-law model

An empirical model is built directly from measured data rather than derived from first principles. Here, the cutting force is expressed as a product of power‑law terms in cutting speed, depth of cut, and feed rate. Taking logarithms of both sides converts the nonlinear relation into a linear problem that ordinary least squares regression solves in a single step.

The result is a compact, interpretable formula that quantifies exactly how much each process parameter contributes to the force, and that can be evaluated instantly for any operating point within the experimental range.

Dynamic plant

The plant is the mathematical model of the physical system the controller must regulate. Here it captures two time‑scale effects: the cutting process itself, which responds to feed changes with a time constant of around 23 ms, and the CNC servo actuator with a 170 ms lag. Together they form a second‑order transfer function that the PSSMC stabilises.

Sliding mode control

Sliding mode control drives the system state onto a designer‑chosen surface in state space and keeps it there with a switching control action. Because the switching is faster than the system dynamics, the closed‑loop behaviour on the sliding surface is largely independent of parameter variations and disturbances — exactly the robustness needed for a chatter‑prone machining process.

The predictive smoothed variant adds a preview horizon that anticipates vibration growth before it becomes severe, and replaces discontinuous switching with a smooth hyperbolic tangent to reduce actuator wear.
