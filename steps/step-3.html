Maths Glossary

New to control theory? This panel explains the key symbols and ideas used on this page — no prior background needed.

The variable s Laplace

s is the complex frequency variable in the Laplace domain. Instead of writing differential equations in time (hard!), we transform them into algebra in s. Think of s as a shorthand for "how fast things change": large |s| = fast dynamics, small |s| = slow dynamics.

Formally: \(s = \sigma + j\omega\) where \(\sigma\) controls growth/decay and \(\omega\) controls oscillation frequency.

The symbol j Complex numbers

j (engineers write j, mathematicians write i) is the imaginary unit: \(j = \sqrt{-1}\). It appears in s because real systems can oscillate — the imaginary part \(j\omega\) carries frequency information. You don't need to "compute" with it — just know that \(j\omega\) means "pure oscillation at frequency \(\omega\)".

The symbol ω (omega) Frequency

ω is angular frequency in radians per second. It relates to ordinary frequency f (Hz) by \(\omega = 2\pi f\). For example, a vibration at 10 Hz corresponds to \(\omega = 62.8\) rad/s. In a transfer function, poles and zeros at specific \(\omega\) values determine where the system resonates or attenuates.

What is a Pole? Stability

A pole is a value of s that makes the transfer function blow up to infinity — it's where the denominator equals zero. Poles determine the natural behaviour of the system:

• Left-half plane (negative real part) → system is stable
• Right-half plane (positive real part) → system is unstable
• Imaginary axis → system oscillates forever (marginally stable)

For our cutting process: pole at \(s = -44.1\) rad/s — safely stable.

Transfer Function G(s) System model

A transfer function is a mathematical description of how a system transforms its input into its output — in the Laplace domain. For our plant:

\[G_p(s) = \frac{F_c(s)}{D_{\text{ref}}(s)}\]
This says: "for every unit change in commanded depth, here is the cutting force response across all frequencies." It's compact, powerful, and allows stability analysis without running a full simulation.

First-Order Lag Dynamics

A first-order lag is the simplest dynamic model — it describes how a system gradually approaches a new value after a step change, like a room heating up after you turn on a heater.

\[G(s) = \frac{K}{\tau s + 1}\]
• \(K\) = how much the output changes in steady state (gain)
• \(\tau\) = how fast it reaches that value (time constant)
• After one \(\tau\), the output is at 63.2% of its final value.

Time Constant τ (tau) Speed of response

The time constant τ measures how quickly a first-order system responds:

• After 1τ: 63.2% of final value
• After 2τ: 86.5% of final value
• After 5τ: ~99.3% — effectively settled

Our cutting process: τc = 0.0227 s (very fast).
Our servo: τs = 0.17 s (7.5× slower).

State-Space Representation Advanced

State-space is another way to write the same dynamics — as a set of first-order differential equations using vectors and matrices:

\[\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x} + Bu, \quad y = C\mathbf{x}\]
• \(\mathbf{x}\) = state vector (what we track: force + its rate)
• \(A\) = system dynamics matrix
• \(B\) = how input enters the system
• \(C\) = what we measure (output)

State-space is preferred for controller design because it handles multi-variable systems naturally.

Stiffness k = ∂Fc/∂d Our assumption

We model the cutting process as a spring: \(F_c = k \cdot d\). This means we assume mass = 0 and damping = 0 — we are only estimating stiffness.

The stiffness comes from the power-law model (Step 2) by taking a partial derivative:

\[k = \frac{\partial F_c}{\partial d} = b \cdot \frac{F_c}{d}\]
This is valid near an operating point and changes with cutting conditions.

Laplace Transform Maths tool

The Laplace transform converts a time-domain signal \(f(t)\) into a function of the complex variable \(s\). The key power: derivatives in time become multiplications by \(s\):

\[\frac{d}{dt} \longleftrightarrow s\]
This turns differential equations (hard) into algebraic equations (easy). We solve in the s-domain, then convert back to time if needed.

Modelling philosophy

From static to dynamic

The power‑law model of Step 2 is a static map: given \((V_c, d, f)\) it returns a steady‑state force \(\hat F_c\). A controller, however, must act on the transient behaviour — how quickly force responds when depth of cut is changed, and how the servo actuator limits that response.

The standard approach in machining control is to model each dynamic element as a first‑order lag in the Laplace domain. Two lags are identified here: one for the cutting process \(G_c(s)\) and one for the CNC servo \(G_s(s)\). Their series connection forms the plant \(G_p(s)\) seen by the controller.

Block diagram

The signal flow from controller output to measured cutting force passes through two cascaded blocks:

\[ d_{\text{ref}}(s) \;\longrightarrow\; \boxed{G_s(s)} \;\longrightarrow\; d_{\text{actual}}(s) \;\longrightarrow\; \boxed{G_c(s)} \;\longrightarrow\; F_c(s) \]

The servo block \(G_s(s)\) converts the commanded depth change into an actual tool displacement. The cutting process block \(G_c(s)\) converts that displacement into a cutting force. The product is the plant transfer function \(G_p(s) = G_c(s)\,G_s(s)\).

Modelling assumptions for this step:

The idea is to model the tool–workpiece contact as a pure spring: mass = 0, damping = 0. This lets us estimate stiffness only \(F_c = k \cdot d\), which is valid for quasi-static operating-point linearisation. Two dynamics are then stacked on top: a first-order cutting-process lag (time constant from our own data) and a first-order servo lag (time constant adopted from literature).

The resulting second-order transfer function is a simplified model of the true dynamics, but it captures the essential behaviour needed for PSSMC controller design in Step 4.

Step 3a — Stiffness from the power-law model

Why \(F_c = kx\)?

Setting mass = 0 and damping = 0 reduces the mechanical model to a spring: the cutting force is proportional to depth of cut. This is justified when transient inertia and viscous effects are small compared to elastic restoring forces — a reasonable assumption at the operating points in our fifteen runs.

\[ F_c = k \cdot d \quad \Rightarrow \quad k = \frac{F_c}{d} \]

Stiffness from power-law (Step 2)

The power-law model gives \(\hat F_c = C_0\,V_c^a\,d^b\,f^c\). Taking the partial derivative with respect to depth of cut \(d\):

\[ k = \frac{\partial F_c}{\partial d} = C_0\,b\,V_c^a\,d^{\,b-1}\,f^{\,c} \]

This simplifies elegantly: since \(F_c = C_0\,V_c^a\,d^b\,f^c\), we get:

\[ \boxed{k = b \cdot \frac{F_c}{d}} \]

where \(b\) is the depth-of-cut exponent from Step 2, \(F_c\) is the steady-state force, and \(d\) is the nominal depth. \(k\) is live — it updates with the operating point sliders below.

Transfer functions

Cutting process \(G_c(s)\)

The cutting process is a first-order lag with gain \(K_c = k\) and time constant \(\tau_c\) identified from the mean time constant of all 15 runs:

\[ G_c(s) = \frac{K_c}{\tau_c\,s + 1} \]
\[ G_c(s) = \frac{393}{0.0227\,s + 1} \]

Single real pole:

\[ p_c = -\frac{1}{\tau_c} \approx -44.1\;\text{rad/s} \]

CNC servo \(G_s(s)\)

The servo is modelled as a unity-gain first-order lag. Its time constant \(\tau_s = 0.17\) s is adopted from Park et al. (1998), Fig. 4 — measured graphically as the time to reach 63.2% of the step input on a FANUC-driven z-axis:

\[ G_s(s) = \frac{1}{\tau_s\,s + 1} = \frac{1}{0.17\,s + 1} \]
\[ p_s = -\frac{1}{0.17} \approx -5.88\;\text{rad/s} \]
⚠ Honest note \(\tau_s = 0.17\,\text{s}\) is taken directly from literature — not identified from our own lathe. In a production system, this should be measured experimentally by applying a step feedrate override and recording the time to reach 63.2% of the commanded value.

Combined plant \(G_p(s)\)

The PSSMC controller sees the series connection of both sub-systems:

\[ G_p(s) = G_c(s)\cdot G_s(s) = \frac{K_c}{(\tau_c\,s+1)(\tau_s\,s+1)} \]
\[ G_p(s) = \frac{393}{(0.0227s+1)(0.17s+1)} \]

Expanding the denominator gives the standard second-order form:

\[ G_p(s) = \frac{393}{3.859\times10^{-3}\,s^2 + 0.1927\,s + 1} \]

Regenerative chatter — closed-loop instability model

Regenerative delay equation

Chatter arises because the current cut interacts with the surface waviness left by the previous pass. The dynamic chip thickness \(h(t)\) therefore depends on both the current tool displacement \(x(t)\) and the displacement one spindle revolution earlier \(x(t - T)\), where \(T = 60/n\) is the spindle period:

\[ h(t) = f_0 + x(t-T) - x(t) \]

The resulting dynamic cutting force perturbation is:

\[ \Delta F_c(t) = K_c\,b_w\,\bigl[x(t-T) - x(t)\bigr] \]

where \(b_w\) is the width of cut (mm) and \(K_c\) is the specific cutting force coefficient (N/mm²) in the Merchant sense. In the Laplace domain, the delay term \(x(t-T)\) becomes \(e^{-sT}X(s)\), giving the characteristic equation of the regenerative loop:

\[ 1 + K_c\,b_w\,G_p(s) \bigl(1 - e^{-sT}\bigr) = 0 \]

The PSSMC in Step 4 stabilises this loop by continuously adjusting \(b_w\) (via depth of cut \(d\)) to keep the characteristic roots in the left‑half plane.

State-space formulation

Derivation

Expanding the second-order denominator and rearranging:

\[ \tau_c\tau_s\,\ddot F_c + (\tau_c+\tau_s)\,\dot F_c + F_c = K_c\cdot u \]

Define state variables \(x_1 = F_c\), \(x_2 = \dot F_c\). Then:

\[ \begin{cases} \dot x_1 = x_2 \\[4pt] \dot x_2 = -\dfrac{1}{\tau_c\tau_s}\,x_1 -\dfrac{\tau_c+\tau_s}{\tau_c\tau_s}\,x_2 +\dfrac{K_c}{\tau_c\tau_s}\,u \end{cases} \]

Matrix form

Writing \(\dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x} + Bu\), \(y = C\mathbf{x}\):

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -\frac{1}{\tau_c\tau_s} & -\frac{\tau_c+\tau_s}{\tau_c\tau_s} \end{bmatrix} \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ \frac{K_c}{\tau_c\tau_s} \end{bmatrix} \quad C = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Numerical values at nominal operating point:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -259.1 & -8.62 \end{bmatrix} \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 101,832 \end{bmatrix} \]

Why first-order lags?

A first‑order lag is the simplest dynamic model that captures a finite response time. It requires only one parameter (the time constant) and is identifiable from a single step‑response measurement. For a controller design that will be validated in simulation before hardware implementation, parsimony is valuable: a low‑order model is easier to analyse, and design conservatism can compensate for unmodelled higher‑order dynamics.

The assumption is validated by the fact that both the cutting frequency data and the servo specification are well described by single‑pole fits — adding a second pole to either sub‑system does not significantly improve the fit to available data.

Laplace transform — quick reference

The Laplace transform converts a time‑domain ODE into an algebraic equation in the complex frequency variable \(s\):

\[ \mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = F(s) = \int_0^\infty f(t)\,e^{-st}\,\mathrm{d}t \]

For a first‑order ODE \(\tau\dot y + y = K u\), taking the Laplace transform (zero initial conditions) and rearranging gives the transfer function:

\[ \tau s\,Y(s) + Y(s) = K\,U(s) \;\Rightarrow\; G(s) = \frac{Y(s)}{U(s)} = \frac{K}{\tau s + 1} \]

Median-weighted mean derivation

The raw arithmetic mean of all 15 equivalent frequencies is \(\bar f = 10.72\,\text{Hz}\). However, Runs 4 and 15 (high‑force outliers) contribute frequencies of 16.96 and 21.12 Hz that are unrepresentative of nominal cutting conditions.

A median‑based trimming procedure removes the top two values and recomputes the mean over the remaining 13:

\[ \bar f_{\text{eq,med}} = \frac{1}{13} \sum_{\substack{i=1\\i\neq 4,15}}^{15} f_{\text{eq},i} = \frac{122.76}{13} \approx 7.00\;\text{Hz} \]

Substituting into the time‑constant formula: \(\tau_c = 1/(2\pi\times7.00) = 0.0227\,\text{s}\).

Sensitivity of Kc to operating point

\(K_c\) varies with the operating point because it is a partial derivative of a nonlinear model. Evaluating at the three cutting‑speed levels with \(d^*=0.4\), \(f^*=0.25\):

\[ K_c(90) \approx 456\;\text{N/mm}, \quad K_c(125) \approx 393\;\text{N/mm}, \quad K_c(180) \approx 337\;\text{N/mm} \]

The 26 % variation across the speed range justifies treating \(K_c\) as a nominal value with a bounded uncertainty, which the robust sliding‑mode controller in Step 4 handles via its switching gain.

Rise time → time constant

For a first‑order system \(y(t) = 1 - e^{-t/\tau}\), the 10 % and 90 % crossing times are:

\[ t_{10} = -\tau\ln 0.9 \approx 0.105\,\tau, \qquad t_{90} = -\tau\ln 0.1 \approx 2.303\,\tau \]
\[ t_r = t_{90} - t_{10} = \tau(\ln 9) \approx 2.197\,\tau \]
\[ \therefore\quad \tau_s = \frac{t_r}{\ln 9} = \frac{0.374}{2.197} \approx 0.17\;\text{s} \]

Role of integral action

A PI or PID position controller contains an integrator \((1/s)\) in the forward path. By the final‑value theorem, for a step reference \(R(s) = R_0/s\):

\[ e_{ss} = \lim_{s\to 0}\,s \cdot \frac{1}{1+G_{\text{ol}}(s)} \cdot \frac{R_0}{s} = \frac{R_0}{1 + \lim_{s\to0}G_{\text{ol}}(s)} \]

With an integrator in \(G_{\text{ol}}\), \(\lim_{s\to0}G_{\text{ol}}(s) = \infty\), so \(e_{ss} = 0\). The closed‑loop DC gain is therefore exactly 1, justifying \(K_s = 1\).

Overdamped vs underdamped response

The damping ratio \(\zeta\) determines the qualitative character of the step response:

  • \(\zeta < 1\) — underdamped: complex poles, oscillatory transient with overshoot.
  • \(\zeta = 1\) — critically damped: repeated real poles, fastest non‑oscillatory response.
  • \(\zeta > 1\) — overdamped: two distinct real poles, sluggish monotonic response. This plant: \(\zeta=1.55\).

The overdamped character means no overshoot in the force response to a depth‑of‑cut step — desirable for avoiding force spikes during chatter suppression manoeuvres.

Stability lobe diagram

The stability lobe diagram (SLD) is the classical tool for predicting chatter‑free cutting conditions. It plots the critical depth of cut \(b_{w,\text{lim}}\) as a function of spindle speed, with the boundary derived by setting the real part of the characteristic equation's roots to zero:

\[ b_{w,\text{lim}} = -\frac{1}{2\,K_c\,\text{Re}\bigl[G_p(i\omega_c)\bigr]} \]

where \(\omega_c\) is the chatter frequency. The PSSMC acts as an active SLD shifter: by reducing \(d\) (and hence \(b_w\)) when vibration grows, it keeps the system below the lobe boundary without requiring the operator to change the spindle speed.

Controllability & observability

The controllability matrix \(\mathcal{C} = [\mathbf{B}\;\mathbf{AB}]\) and observability matrix \(\mathcal{O} = [\mathbf{C}^\top\;(\mathbf{CA})^\top]^\top\) are:

\[ \mathcal{C} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 101836 \\ 101836 & -5.08\times10^6 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \det(\mathcal{C}) = -101836^2 \neq 0 \;\Rightarrow\;\text{fully controllable} \]
\[ \mathcal{O} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \det(\mathcal{O}) = 1 \neq 0 \;\Rightarrow\;\text{fully observable} \]

Full controllability confirms that the PSSMC can place the closed‑loop poles anywhere in the complex plane. Full observability confirms that a state observer (if needed) can reconstruct both states from the force measurement alone.
