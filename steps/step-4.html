PSSMC Controller Design

The Predictive Smoothed Sliding Mode Controller is the central intellectual contribution of this research. Built in a deliberate sequence — sliding surface, equivalent control, smooth switching, predictive extension, and three adaptive mechanisms — the result is a controller whose behaviour is both mathematically rigorous and physically intuitive.

4.0 Overview

The central question

Given that regenerative chatter arises from the self‑reinforcing interaction between tool vibration and the surface left by the previous cut — can a controller be designed that not only reacts to chatter when it appears, but anticipates it before it grows dangerous, and does so efficiently without wasting computational resources during calm cutting conditions?

The PSSMC answers that question affirmatively. Its output at every instant is a single scalar: the commanded depth of cut \(d_c(t)\).

Design sequence

The controller is assembled layer by layer. Each layer solves a specific limitation of its predecessor:

4.1 Control objective & error signal

Tracking error definition

The objective of the PSSMC is to drive the actual cutting force \(F_c(t)\) to a desired reference value \(F_{\text{ref}}\) by dynamically adjusting \(d_c(t)\). Cutting force is chosen as the controlled variable because it is the primary physical quantity that mediates between the geometric parameters of the cut and the vibratory dynamics of the tool‑workpiece system.

The tracking error is defined as the signed difference between the desired and actual force:

\[ e(t) = F_{\text{ref}} - F_c(t) \]

Since \(F_{\text{ref}}\) is treated as a constant operating setpoint, the time derivative of the error reduces to:

\[ \dot{e}(t) = \dot{F}_{\text{ref}} - \dot{F}_c(t) = -\dot{F}_c(t) \]

This relationship is used repeatedly in the derivations that follow. Its physical meaning is direct: the rate at which the error changes is precisely the rate at which the cutting force changes, with opposite sign.

4.2 The sliding surface

Construction

Rather than driving the error directly to zero, the SMC approach defines a surface in error space and drives the system onto it. Once on the surface, the error decays to zero automatically. The sliding surface is:

\[ s(t) = \dot{e}(t) + \lambda\,e(t) \]

where \(\lambda > 0\) is the sliding gain. The surface \(s(t) = 0\) is a line in the \((e, \dot{e})\) phase plane with slope \(-\lambda\). Any trajectory that reaches this line and stays on it must satisfy:

\[ \dot{e} = -\lambda\,e \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e(t) = e(0)\,e^{-\lambda t} \]

This is exponential decay. Once on the surface, stability is guaranteed regardless of plant dynamics, cutting parameters, or the disturbance environment.

Interpretation of s(t)

The sign of \(s(t)\) encodes the instantaneous state of the system relative to the surface:

  • \(s(t) > 0\) — system is above the surface; controller pushes it downward toward \(s = 0\)
  • \(s(t) < 0\) — system is below the surface; controller pushes it upward
  • \(s(t) = 0\) — system is on the surface; error is decaying exponentially, no additional forcing needed

The controller's entire task can now be stated with elegant simplicity: keep \(s(t) = 0\) at all times. Everything else — suppression of force deviation, rejection of vibration‑induced disturbances — follows automatically.

4.3 Equivalent control — derivation from first principles

Step-by-step derivation

The equivalent control \(u_{eq}\) maintains the system on the sliding surface once it has reached it. It is derived from the invariance condition \(\dot{s}(t) = 0\):

Step 1 — enforce the surface condition:

\[ \dot{s}(t) = \ddot{e}(t) + \lambda\,\dot{e}(t) = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \dot{e}(t) = -\lambda\,e(t) \]

Substituting \(\dot{e}(t) = -\dot{F}_c(t)\):

\[ \dot{F}_c(t) = \lambda\,e(t) \]

Step 2 — invoke the plant model from Step 3:

\[ \tau_c\,\dot{F}_c(t) + F_c(t) = K_s \cdot d_a(t) \;\Rightarrow\; \dot{F}_c(t) = \frac{K_s\,d_a(t) - F_c(t)}{\tau_c} \]

Step 3 — match required rate to plant rate:

\[ \frac{K_s\,d_{eq}(t) - F_c(t)}{\tau_c} = \lambda\,e(t) \]

Step 4 — solve for the equivalent depth‑of‑cut command:

\[ \boxed{ u_{eq}(t) = d_{eq}(t) = \frac{1}{K_s} \bigl[F_c(t) + \tau_c\,\lambda\,e(t)\bigr] } \]

The two additive terms each have a clear physical role: \(F_c(t)/K_s\) is a force compensation term that counteracts the force the process is currently generating; \(\tau_c\lambda\,e(t)/K_s\) is an error correction term that drives \(F_c(t)\) toward \(F_{\text{ref}}\) at the rate dictated by \(\lambda\).

4.4 Switching control — smooth restoration

Classical sign function & chattering

The standard switching control in classical SMC is:

\[ u_{sw} = K \cdot \text{sign}(s(t)) \]

The sign function is maximally aggressive — it applies the full gain \(K\) in whichever direction is needed, instantaneously switching between \(+K\) and \(-K\) as \(s(t)\) crosses zero. In the neighbourhood of the surface, where \(s(t)\) oscillates rapidly due to measurement noise, this generates high‑frequency switching of the depth‑of‑cut command. In a CNC machine, this chattering excites mechanical resonances, accelerates tool wear, and potentially introduces the very vibrations the controller is designed to suppress.

tanh replacement

The chattering problem is resolved by replacing the discontinuous sign function with the hyperbolic tangent:

\[ u_{sw}(t) = K \cdot \tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t)}{\varepsilon(t)}\right) \]

The tanh function is infinitely differentiable and behaves exactly as needed:

  • \(|s| \gg \varepsilon\): saturates at \(\pm 1\), delivering the full restoring force \(\pm K\)
  • \(|s| \approx \varepsilon\): output decreases smoothly and continuously
  • \(s = 0\): equals exactly zero — no chattering

The parameter \(\varepsilon(t)\) is the boundary layer thickness — it defines the transition zone width. Its adaptive design is presented in §4.6.3.

4.5 Predictive extension — anticipating the surface

The delay problem

The plant model from Step 3 reveals an unavoidable physical reality: the CNC servo introduces a delay of \(\tau_s = 0.17\,\text{s}\) and the cutting process introduces a further \(\tau_c = 0.0227\,\text{s}\) delay. The total delay in the \(d_c \rightarrow F_c\) chain is approximately 0.19 s.

A controller acting only on \(s(t)\) is purely reactive — it responds to what has already happened. During 0.19 s of corrective lag, chatter can grow substantially. The predictive extension transforms the controller into a proactive system that acts on the predicted future surface value \(\hat{s}(t + T_p)\).

Trend-based prediction

Rather than solving the full nonlinear system forward in time, the predicted surface is approximated by a first‑order Taylor expansion:

\[ \boxed{ \hat{s}(t + T_p) = s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\cdot T_p(t) } \]

The surface rate \(\dot{s}(t)\) is estimated from two consecutive samples using a backward finite difference:

\[ \dot{s}(t) \approx \frac{s(t) - s(t-\Delta t)}{\Delta t} \]

This requires storing only the previous sample of \(s\) — a single scalar — making the prediction computationally trivial. Over the short horizons \(T_p \in [0.0075,\, 0.0710]\,\text{s}\) used here, the linear approximation is well‑justified.

4.6 Adaptive parameters — the novel contribution

Standard SMC controllers use fixed gains chosen as a compromise across the operating range. This research resolves that compromise by making three controller parameters continuously adaptive — each driven by a physically meaningful signal and using tanh to ensure smooth, bounded variation consistent with the overall smoothing philosophy.

4.6.1   Adaptive sliding gain \(\lambda(t)\)

The sliding gain \(\lambda\) governs the error decay rate on the surface. The instantaneous rate of change of the sliding surface, \(|\dot{s}(t)|\), is a natural measure of how rapidly the machine's behaviour is evolving. Making \(\lambda\) a continuous, monotonically increasing function of \(|\dot{s}(t)|\) via tanh gives:

\[ \boxed{ \lambda(t) = \lambda_{\min} + (\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min}) \cdot \tanh\!\left(\frac{|\dot{s}(t)|}{\sigma}\right) } \]

Since \(\lambda\) is physically the inverse of the cutting process time constant, its bounds are derived directly from the 15 experimental runs:

\[ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{1}{\tau_c^{\max}} = \frac{1}{0.0710} \approx 14.1\;\text{s}^{-1} \quad\text{(Run 7 — slowest dynamics)} \] \[ \lambda_{\max} = \frac{1}{\tau_c^{\min}} = \frac{1}{0.0075} \approx 133.3\;\text{s}^{-1} \quad\text{(Run 15 — fastest dynamics)} \] \[ \sigma = \frac{1}{\tau_c^{\text{mean}}} = \frac{1}{0.0227} \approx 44.1\;\text{s}^{-1} \quad\text{(nominal operating condition)} \]

When \(|\dot{s}|/\sigma \ll 1\): the tanh approaches zero, \(\lambda(t) \approx \lambda_{\min}\) — the controller is gentle. When \(|\dot{s}|/\sigma \gg 1\): tanh saturates, \(\lambda(t) \approx \lambda_{\max}\) — the controller is maximally aggressive. Between these extremes the gain transitions smoothly — never jumping, never saturating abruptly.

4.6.2   Adaptive prediction horizon \(T_p(t)\)

A longer horizon is beneficial when the machine is vibrating strongly; a shorter horizon is appropriate when the machine is calm. The vibration amplitude \(A(t)\) — directly measurable from the accelerometer — is the natural driving variable:

\[ \boxed{ T_p(t) = T_p^{\min} + (T_p^{\max} - T_p^{\min}) \cdot \tanh\!\left(\frac{A(t)}{A_{\text{ref}}}\right) } \]

The bounds are taken directly from the range of cutting process time constants across the 15 runs:

\[ T_p^{\min} = \tau_c^{\min} = 0.0075\;\text{s} \quad\text{(Run 15)}, \qquad T_p^{\max} = \tau_c^{\max} = 0.0710\;\text{s} \quad\text{(Run 7)} \] \[ A_{\text{ref}} = A_{\max} = 0.0066\;\text{m/s} \quad\text{(maximum observed — Run 15)} \]

The adaptive \(T_p\) serves a dual purpose: it improves control performance by matching the prediction depth to the disturbance severity, and it conserves computational resources by keeping the prediction window short during calm cutting.

4.6.3   Adaptive boundary layer \(\varepsilon(t)\)

A thick boundary layer eliminates chattering but allows a nonzero steady‑state error residual; a thin layer achieves high precision but risks reintroducing chattering during heavy vibration. Making \(\varepsilon\) adapt with \(A(t)\) resolves this tension continuously:

\[ \boxed{ \varepsilon(t) = \varepsilon_{\min} + (\varepsilon_{\max} - \varepsilon_{\min}) \cdot \tanh\!\left(\frac{A(t)}{A_{\text{ref}}}\right) } \]

A critical design feature: \(\varepsilon(t)\) and \(T_p(t)\) share the same driving signal \(A(t)\) and the same functional form. They adapt simultaneously and in the same direction — when amplitude grows, both the prediction horizon extends and the boundary layer widens, reinforcing each other in disturbance rejection. When amplitude falls, both contract, restoring precision and efficiency. This architectural consistency is deliberate: all adaptive mechanisms respond to the same ground truth — the instantaneous severity of the vibration environment.

Values of \(\varepsilon_{\min} \in [0.01, 0.05]\) and \(\varepsilon_{\max} \in [0.1, 0.5]\) are to be determined through simulation tuning in Step 5.

4.7 Complete PSSMC control law

Assembled control law

Assembling all components, the complete control law is:

\[ \boxed{ d_c(t) = \underbrace{ \frac{1}{K_s} \bigl[F_c(t) + \tau_c\,\lambda(t)\,e(t)\bigr] }_{\displaystyle u_{eq}(t)\ —\ \text{maintains surface}} +\; \underbrace{ K \cdot \tanh\!\left( \frac{\hat{s}(t + T_p(t))}{\varepsilon(t)} \right) }_{\displaystyle u_{sw}(t)\ —\ \text{restores surface}} } \]

with the three adaptive components:

\[ \lambda(t) = 14.1 + 119.2\cdot\tanh\!\left(\frac{|\dot{s}(t)|}{44.1}\right) \;\;\text{s}^{-1} \] \[ T_p(t) = 0.0075 + 0.0635 \cdot\tanh\!\left(\frac{A(t)}{0.0066}\right) \;\;\text{s} \] \[ \varepsilon(t) = \varepsilon_{\min} + (\varepsilon_{\max}-\varepsilon_{\min}) \cdot\tanh\!\left(\frac{A(t)}{0.0066}\right) \] \[ \hat{s}(t+T_p) = s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\cdot T_p(t) \]

This is a fully specified, closed‑form control law. At every sampling instant, given measurements of \(F_c(t)\) and \(A(t)\) and the stored previous value \(s(t-\Delta t)\), the controller computes \(d_c(t)\) through a sequence of algebraic and elementary operations — no differential equations, no matrix inversions, no iterative solvers.

4.8 Lyapunov stability analysis

Lyapunov candidate function

A simulation demonstrates performance only at the specific conditions tested. A Lyapunov proof provides a mathematical guarantee that holds universally: for any initial condition and any bounded disturbance, the system converges to the neighbourhood of the sliding surface and remains there.

The standard SMC candidate is chosen as:

\[ V(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,s^2(t) \]

This satisfies the first two Lyapunov conditions by inspection:

  • \(V(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}s^2 > 0\) for all \(s \neq 0\) — always positive (Right on track)
  • \(V(0) = 0\) — zero precisely at the surface (Belisimoo!!)

Proof of negative definiteness

Differentiating the candidate:

\[ \dot{V}(s) = s(t)\cdot\dot{s}(t) \]

With the PSSMC law applied, the closed‑loop surface dynamics are:

\[ \dot{s}(t) = -K\cdot\tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t)}{\varepsilon(t)}\right) + \delta(t) \]

where \(|\delta(t)| \leq \Delta\) bounds all disturbances. Applying the key tanh inequality \(s\cdot\tanh(s/\varepsilon) \geq |s| - \varepsilon\ln\cosh(1)\):

\[ \dot{V} \leq -(K-\Delta)|s| + K\varepsilon\ln\cosh(1) \]

The stability condition is therefore:

\[ \boxed{K > \Delta} \]

4.9 Real-time implementation algorithm

Execution sequence per sampling instant \(\Delta t\)

INITIALISE:
  s_prev = 0     // previous surface value
  e_prev = 0     // previous error

AT EACH TIME STEP t:

STEP 1 — MEASURE:
  Read Fc(t)    // cutting force — dynamometer
  Read A(t)     // vibration amplitude — accelerometer

STEP 2 — ERROR:
  e(t) = Fref − Fc(t)

STEP 3 — SURFACE:
  e_dot = [e(t) − e_prev] / Δt
  s(t) = e_dot + λ(t)·e(t)
  s_dot = [s(t) − s_prev] / Δt

STEP 4 — ADAPTIVE PARAMETERS:
  λ(t) = λ_min + (λ_max − λ_min)·tanh(|s_dot|/σ)
  Tp(t) = Tp_min + (Tp_max − Tp_min)·tanh(A(t)/Aref)
  ε(t) = ε_min + (ε_max − ε_min)·tanh(A(t)/Aref)

STEP 5 — PREDICT SURFACE:
  s_hat = s(t) + s_dot·Tp(t)

STEP 6 — COMPUTE CONTROL:
  u_eq = (1/Ks)·[Fc(t) + τc·λ(t)·e(t)]
  u_sw = K·tanh(s_hat / ε(t))
  dc(t) = u_eq + u_sw

STEP 7 — OUTPUT:
  Send dc(t) → CNC Servo → Cutting Process → Fc(t+Δt)

STEP 8 — STORE:
  s_prev = s(t)
  e_prev = e(t)

REPEAT

The algorithm requires no matrix operations, no nonlinear solvers, and no online optimisation. Its computational footprint per sample consists of a fixed number of additions, multiplications, and tanh evaluations — well within the real‑time capability of any modern CNC controller.

4.11 Novel contributions

①
Trend-based predictive surface
Rather than requiring a high‑fidelity forward model, the controller estimates the future sliding surface from the current trend using a first‑order Taylor expansion. This preserves the predictive advantage without the computational cost of full state prediction.
②
Continuous adaptive sliding gain
\(\lambda(t)\) responds continuously to the rate of change of the sliding surface — scaling from gentle correction during smooth cutting to aggressive correction during intense vibration. This eliminates the performance compromise of fixed‑gain designs.
③
Amplitude-driven joint adaptation
\(T_p(t)\) and \(\varepsilon(t)\) are co‑adapted via the same signal and the same functional form, ensuring architectural consistency and enabling simultaneous tuning of anticipation depth and chattering suppression with a single observable input.

Signal chain dc → Fc

The controller output \(d_c(t)\) passes through two physical sub‑systems before producing the measured output \(F_c(t)\):

\[ d_c(t) \;\xrightarrow{\displaystyle G_s(s)}\; d_a(t) \;\xrightarrow{\displaystyle G_c(s)}\; F_c(t) \]

The total delay in this chain is \(\tau_c + \tau_s = 0.0227 + 0.17 \approx 0.19\,\text{s}\). The controller does not act on feed rate — it controls depth of cut directly, continuously, and in real time.

Phase plane geometry of the sliding surface

The \((e, \dot{e})\) phase plane is the two‑dimensional state space of the error dynamics. The sliding surface \(s = \dot{e} + \lambda e = 0\) is a straight line through the origin with slope \(-\lambda\). Trajectories above the line (positive \(s\)) are pushed downward; trajectories below (negative \(s\)) are pushed upward. Once any trajectory reaches the line, it follows it to the origin at the exponential rate \(e^{-\lambda t}\).

\[ \text{On the surface: } \frac{d\dot{e}}{de} = \frac{-\lambda\,e}{e} = -\lambda \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{slope} = -\lambda \]

Physical interpretation of ueq

The equivalent control \(u_{eq} = \frac{1}{K_s}[F_c(t) + \tau_c\lambda\,e(t)]\) has two additive terms with distinct roles:

  • \(F_c(t)/K_s\) — force compensation: commands a depth of cut proportional to the current force, effectively neutralising whatever force the process is generating and preventing it from causing the system to drift.
  • \(\tau_c\lambda\,e(t)/K_s\) — error correction: commands an additional increment proportional to the current tracking error, driving \(F_c(t)\) toward \(F_{\text{ref}}\) at the rate dictated by \(\lambda\).

Together, these terms ensure that on the sliding surface, the cutting force tracks the reference exactly and the error decays at the prescribed exponential rate.

Why chattering is harmful in CNC turning

Chattering in a depth‑of‑cut controller is particularly destructive because the CNC servo actuator has finite mechanical bandwidth. Rapidly alternating commands between \(+K\) and \(-K\) at kHz rates excite ball‑screw resonances, cause premature bearing wear, and generate periodic force variations that are indistinguishable from chatter itself — making the controller the primary source of the vibration it is supposed to suppress. The tanh replacement eliminates this pathology by guaranteeing that \(u_{sw} = 0\) exactly when \(s = 0\).

Accuracy of the Taylor prediction

The first‑order Taylor expansion \(\hat{s}(t+T_p) \approx s(t) + \dot{s}(t)T_p\) incurs a truncation error of order \(O(T_p^2)\):

\[ \hat{s}(t+T_p) - s(t+T_p) = \tfrac{1}{2}\ddot{s}(\xi)\,T_p^2 \quad \xi \in (t,\, t+T_p) \]

For \(T_p^{\max} = 0.071\,\text{s}\), the error is of order \(0.071^2 / 2 \approx 2.5 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s}^2\) times the second derivative of \(s\). Since \(\ddot{s}\) remains bounded by the physical constraints of the cutting process, the approximation is well‑justified over the horizon ranges used in this study.

Practical stability vs asymptotic stability

Asymptotic stability requires \(s(t) \to 0\) exactly as \(t \to \infty\). The tanh boundary layer prevents this: inside the layer (\(|s| \leq \varepsilon\)), the restoring force is modulated and the system state may settle at a small nonzero \(s\).

Practical stability (ultimate boundedness) is weaker but sufficient: the state converges to and remains within a compact neighbourhood of the surface whose radius shrinks as \(\varepsilon \to 0\). In chatter suppression, the controller does not need to drive the force error to exactly zero — it needs to keep the deviation small enough that the regenerative mechanism cannot sustain itself. Practical stability is precisely the right guarantee for this application.

Computational cost per sample

Each execution of the algorithm requires exactly:

  • 2 divisions, 6 multiplications, 8 additions
  • 3 tanh evaluations (each equivalent to ~20 FLOPs on modern hardware)
  • 1 absolute value, 2 scalar stores

Total: approximately 80–100 floating‑point operations. At a 1 kHz sampling rate, this requires roughly 0.1 MFLOPS — orders of magnitude below the capability of any industrial PLC or DSP used in CNC control since 2010.
