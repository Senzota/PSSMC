Step 2 of 5

Empirical Power-Law Model

The fifteen cutting‑force measurements from Step 1 are used to fit a three‑parameter power‑law model by ordinary least squares regression in logarithmic space. The result is a compact analytical expression that quantifies exactly how cutting speed, depth of cut, and feed rate each shape the cutting force — and that becomes the static gain of the dynamic plant in Step 3.

← Step 1 Step 3 →

Model form

Power-law force equation

The cutting force is expressed as a product of power‑law terms — a form first proposed by Kienzle (1952) and widely used in empirical machining studies because it is parsimonious, physically interpretable, and linearisable:

\[ F_c = C_0 \cdot V_c^{\,a} \cdot d^{\,b} \cdot f^{\,c} \]

where \(C_0\) is the force coefficient (units of N when \(V_c\) is in m/min, \(d\) and \(f\) in mm), and \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) are dimensionless exponents that capture the sensitivity of force to each parameter.

Linearisation by logarithm

Taking the natural logarithm of both sides converts the nonlinear product into a linear model in the unknowns \(\ln C_0\), \(a\), \(b\), \(c\):

\[ \ln F_c = \ln C_0 + a\ln V_c + b\ln d + c\ln f \]

Defining \(Y = \ln F_c\), \(\beta_0 = \ln C_0\), \(x_1 = \ln V_c\), \(x_2 = \ln d\), \(x_3 = \ln f\):

\[ Y = \beta_0 + a\,x_1 + b\,x_2 + c\,x_3 \]

This is now a standard multiple linear regression problem solvable by OLS in a single matrix step.

Ordinary least squares derivation

Matrix formulation

Stack the \(n = 15\) log‑transformed observations into a design matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) and response vector \(\mathbf{y}\):

\[ \mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} \ln F_{c,1} \\ \ln F_{c,2} \\ \vdots \\ \ln F_{c,15} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{X} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \ln V_{c,1} & \ln d_1 & \ln f_1 \\ 1 & \ln V_{c,2} & \ln d_2 & \ln f_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \\ 1 & \ln V_{c,15} & \ln d_{15} & \ln f_{15} \end{bmatrix} \]

The OLS estimator minimises the sum of squared residuals \(\|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta}\|^2\):

\[ \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \bigl(\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X}\bigr)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y} \]

where \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = [\hat\beta_0,\,\hat a,\,\hat b,\,\hat c]^\top\). Back‑transforming the intercept gives the force coefficient: \(\hat C_0 = e^{\hat\beta_0}\).

Final fitted model

Explicit force prediction equation

Substituting the fitted coefficients back into the power‑law form gives the prediction equation used throughout this study:

\[ \boxed{ \hat{F}_c = 4838.94 \cdot V_c^{-0.3024} \cdot d^{\;0.5436} \cdot f^{\;0.8046} } \]

with \(V_c\) in m/min, \(d\) in mm, \(f\) in mm/rev, and \(\hat{F}_c\) in N. This equation is valid within the experimental range: \(V_c \in [90, 180]\), \(d \in [0.1, 0.8]\), \(f \in [0.125, 0.5]\).

Extrapolation beyond these bounds is not recommended without additional validation, because the thermal and tribological mechanisms that govern cutting force may change character outside the calibration window.

Goodness-of-fit analysis

R² in log-space

The coefficient of determination is computed in log‑space because that is the space in which the regression was performed:

\[ R^2 = 1 - \frac{ \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{15} \bigl(\ln F_{c,i} - \ln\hat F_{c,i}\bigr)^2 }{ \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{15} \bigl(\ln F_{c,i} - \overline{\ln F_c}\bigr)^2 } = 0.881 \]

An \(R^2\) of 0.881 is considered acceptable for an empirical machining model with only three predictors spanning a wide parameter space. The remaining 11.9 % of unexplained variance is attributed to tool wear progression, thermal drift between runs, and measurement noise in the dynamometer signal.

Residual analysis

The log‑space residuals \(e_i = \ln F_{c,i} - \ln\hat F_{c,i}\) are checked for the three OLS assumptions:

  • Zero mean: \(\bar{e} \approx 0\) — satisfied by construction of the OLS normal equations.
  • Homoscedasticity: residual magnitude shows no strong trend with \(\hat Y\) — approximately satisfied.
  • Normality: with only 15 points a formal Shapiro‑Wilk test has low power, but visual inspection of the quantile plot shows no severe departures.

Parameter sensitivity

Elasticity interpretation of exponents

Because the model is a power law, the fitted exponents are directly interpretable as elasticities — the percentage change in \(F_c\) per 1 % change in each input, holding the others constant:

\[ \varepsilon_{F_c, x_i} = \frac{\partial \ln F_c}{\partial \ln x_i} = \text{exponent of } x_i \]

Applied to the three parameters:

\[ \varepsilon_{F_c, V_c} = -0.3024 \quad\Rightarrow\quad +1\%\,V_c \;\Longrightarrow\; -0.30\%\,F_c \] \[ \varepsilon_{F_c, d} = +0.5436 \quad\Rightarrow\quad +1\%\,d \;\Longrightarrow\; +0.54\%\,F_c \] \[ \varepsilon_{F_c, f} = +0.8046 \quad\Rightarrow\quad +1\%\,f \;\Longrightarrow\; +0.80\%\,F_c \]

Feed rate \(f\) is therefore the most influential parameter (largest absolute elasticity), followed by depth of cut \(d\), while cutting speed \(V_c\) has the weakest effect and acts in the opposite direction. This ranking directly informs the choice of depth of cut as the control variable in Step 4: it offers the most direct authority over force after feed rate, and unlike feed rate it can be modulated mid‑cut without altering the chip‑thickness kinematics.

Kienzle's cutting force model (1952)

Otto Kienzle proposed expressing the specific cutting force \(k_c\) as a function of chip thickness \(h\) and width \(b\):

\[ k_c = k_{c1} \cdot h^{-m_c} \]

where \(k_{c1}\) is the specific force at unit chip thickness and \(m_c\) is the Kienzle exponent. The total cutting force is then \(F_c = k_c \cdot b \cdot h\). The three‑parameter power‑law used in this study is a generalisation of Kienzle's form that also accounts for cutting speed effects.

Why log-linearise?

Direct nonlinear least squares optimisation of the power‑law requires an iterative solver that may converge to a local minimum. Log‑linearisation converts the problem into a globally optimal linear solve — the OLS normal equations have a unique closed‑form solution \((\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}\) that is guaranteed to be the global minimiser of the sum of squared log‑space residuals.

The trade‑off is that OLS in log‑space minimises relative errors (percent deviations) rather than absolute errors in Newtons. For force prediction this is usually preferable because a 30 N error at 100 N is more consequential than a 30 N error at 500 N.

OLS normal equations — proof

The OLS objective is to minimise the residual sum of squares:

\[ S(\boldsymbol\beta) = \|\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol\beta\|^2 = (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol\beta)^\top (\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol\beta) \]

Expanding and differentiating with respect to \(\boldsymbol\beta\), setting the gradient to zero:

\[ \frac{\partial S}{\partial\boldsymbol\beta} = -2\mathbf{X}^\top(\mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol\beta) = \mathbf{0} \]
\[ \Rightarrow\quad \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\,\hat{\boldsymbol\beta} = \mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y} \]

These are the normal equations. When \(\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X}\) is invertible (full column rank, guaranteed here since no two columns are linearly dependent), the unique solution is \(\hat{\boldsymbol\beta} = (\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{X})^{-1}\mathbf{X}^\top\mathbf{y}\).

Log-space residuals

The residuals \(e_i = \ln F_{c,i} - \ln\hat F_{c,i}\) for each run, rounded to three decimal places:

\[ \mathbf{e} \approx [0.04,\,-0.18,\,0.07,\,0.19,\,0.15,\,-0.62,\,-0.12, \,-0.59,\,0.06,\,-0.14,\,-0.05,\,0.07,\,0.04,\,0.08,\,0.10] \]

The two large negative residuals correspond to Runs 6 and 8 (centre‑point repeats with anomalously low measured forces). Removing them raises R² to approximately 0.94, suggesting these runs experienced favourable cutting conditions not captured by the three‑parameter model.

Why control depth of cut?

Three candidate actuated variables were considered: spindle speed (changing \(V_c\)), feed override (changing \(f\)), and depth‑of‑cut override (changing \(d\)).

Feed rate was rejected because reducing \(f\) also changes chip thickness, which alters the regenerative delay and complicates stability analysis. Spindle speed has the weakest force elasticity (\(-0.30\)) and acts in the wrong direction for chatter suppression. Depth of cut has a direct, positive, moderate elasticity (\(+0.54\)) and can be reduced continuously by the CNC servo without perturbing the chip‑thickness kinematics — making it the most suitable control input.

Validity & limitations

The model is valid for AISI 1045 steel with a new or lightly worn carbide insert, dry cutting, and parameters within the experimental ranges. Key limitations:

  • Tool wear is not a model input — the coefficients represent an average across the wear states present during the 15 runs.
  • The model is static — it predicts steady‑state force, not the transient force during chatter oscillation.
  • No interaction terms are included; the model cannot capture combined effects of, say, high feed and high depth of cut that deviate from the product‑of‑powers form.
