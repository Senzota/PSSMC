Step 1 of 5

Experimental Cutting Data

A conventional lathe cannot suppress chatter on its own — but the data it produces can be used to build a mathematical model of the cutting process. That model is then simulated to represent the behaviour of a servo‑equipped machine, and a Predictive Smoothed Sliding Mode Controller is designed and tested entirely within that simulation. This page documents the fifteen turning experiments that start that journey.

Workpiece & machine

The workpiece material is medium‑carbon steel (C = 0.3509 %, S = 0.0092 %) in the form of a round bar 47 mm diameter × 150 mm long. All cuts were performed on a conventional lathe without cutting fluid, using a carbide insert tool and tool‑holder assembly to keep tool‑geometry effects constant across all fifteen runs.

Vibration was measured by placing a vibration meter at the surface of the cutting tool mounted on the tool post. The three‑component force was recorded for each run.

Face-centred design

The experiment follows a face‑centred central composite design (FCCCD). For three factors each at three levels this places runs at:

  • 8 corner points (all combinations of −1 and +1)
  • 6 face‑centre points (one factor at centre, others at ±1)
  • 1 centre point (all factors at 0)

This gives 15 distinct operating conditions that provide good coverage of the design space with enough degrees of freedom to estimate all main effects and two‑factor interactions.

Force component definitions

Three-force model

In orthogonal turning, the resultant cutting force \(\mathbf{F}\) is resolved into three mutually perpendicular components acting at the tool tip:

\[ \mathbf{F} = F_c\,\hat{e}_c + F_f\,\hat{e}_f + F_p\,\hat{e}_p \]

\(F_c\) — cutting force (tangential): This force acts in the direction of primary cutting motion and It is the largest component and the one modelled in Step 2.

\(F_f\) — feed force (axial): typically 30–50 % of \(F_c\).

\(F_p\) — passive force (radial): acts perpendicular to the machined surface. More over this is the primary driver of tool deflection and regenerative chatter.

Equivalent vibration frequency

The vibration signal is processed to yield an equivalent frequency \(f_{\text{eq}}\) — the dominant spectral peak of the vibration signal for each run. From this, the cutting‑process time constant for each run is computed as:

\[ \tau_c^{(i)} = \frac{1}{2\pi\,f_{\text{eq},i}} \]

The mean time constant across all 15 runs:

\[ \bar{\tau}_c = \frac{1}{15}\sum_{i=1}^{15}\tau_c^{(i)} \approx 0.0227\;\text{s} \]

This value feeds directly into the dynamic plant model of Step 3. Note: averaging the time constants directly (not the frequencies) gives the correct mean.

Spindle speed derivation

From cutting speed to spindle speed

The lathe accepts spindle speed \(n\) in rev/min, not cutting speed \(V_c\) in m/min. The conversion uses the workpiece diameter \(D = 47\,\text{mm}\):

\[ n = \frac{1000\,V_c}{\pi\,D} \]

The three cutting‑speed levels map to:

\[ n_{90} = \frac{1000 \times 90 }{\pi \times 47} \approx 610\;\text{rev/min} \] \[ n_{125} = \frac{1000 \times 125}{\pi \times 47} \approx 847\;\text{rev/min} \] \[ n_{180} = \frac{1000 \times 180}{\pi \times 47} \approx 1219\;\text{rev/min} \]

Key observations

Force trends

  • Feed rate \(f\) has the strongest positive effect — doubling \(f\) roughly doubles \(F_c\).
  • Depth of cut \(d\) raises \(F_c\) sub‑linearly — doubling \(d\) increases \(F_c\) by roughly 45 %.
  • Cutting speed \(V_c\) works in reverse — higher speed reduces force through thermal softening of the chip.

Time constant range

The individual time constants span from 0.00754 s (Run 15 — highest frequency, fastest dynamics) to 0.07105 s (Run 7 — lowest frequency, slowest dynamics) — a ten‑fold range.

The mean time constant:

\[ \bar{\tau}_c = \frac{1}{15}\sum_{i=1}^{15} \frac{1}{2\pi\,f_{\text{eq},i}} \approx 0.0227\;\text{s} \]

This ten‑fold range across operating conditions is precisely why the PSSMC uses an adaptive sliding gain \(\lambda(t)\) in Step 4 — a fixed gain cannot serve the full range.

Handover to Step 2: the four columns Vc, \(f\), \(d\), and Fc are taken into the OLS regression that builds the power‑law force model. The \(\tau_c^{(i)}\) column — and its mean 0.0227 s — is reserved for the dynamic plant model in Step 3. Ff and Fp are archived for completeness but are not used in the modelling pipeline.

Research Framework

A conventional lathe cannot suppress chatter autonomously. This research uses experimental data from such a machine to construct a mathematical model — then designs and validates a controller entirely within simulation, representing the behaviour of a servo‑equipped machine.

1
Experimental Data — Conventional Lathe 15 turning runs on medium‑carbon steel. Cutting force, vibration amplitude, and equivalent frequency recorded for each combination of speed, feed, and depth of cut. No servo. No automation. Raw physical data.
2
Power-Law Force Model — OLS Regression The 15 data points are fitted to a power‑law model \(\hat F_c = C_0\,V_c^a\,d^b\,f^c\) using ordinary least squares. This gives the static relationship between cutting parameters and cutting force.
3
Dynamic Plant Model — First-Order Transfer Functions The static model is lifted into the time domain. The cutting process time constant \(\bar\tau_c = 0.0227\,\text{s}\) is derived from the experimental frequencies. A CNC servo time constant \(\tau_s = 0.17\,\text{s}\) is introduced to represent the servo‑equipped machine in simulation. Together they form the second‑order plant \(G_p(s)\).
4
PSSMC Controller Design A Predictive Smoothed Sliding Mode Controller is derived from first principles — sliding surface, equivalent control, tanh switching, predictive horizon, and three adaptive parameters. Lyapunov stability is proved analytically.
5
MATLAB / Simulink Simulation & Validation The PSSMC is closed around the plant model in Simulink. Performance under regenerative chatter excitation is compared against conventional SMC and PID controllers. Three tuning parameters are finalised through simulation.

Force & vibration measurement

Cutting force components were recorded using a three‑component measurement setup mounted between the tool holder and the lathe. The three components — tangential \(F_c\), axial \(F_f\), and radial \(F_p\) — were resolved simultaneously for each run.

Vibration was measured by placing a vibration meter directly at the surface of the cutting tool on the tool post. The dominant vibration frequency for each run was extracted and recorded as the equivalent frequency \(f_{\text{eq}}\).

Face-centred CCD geometry

In coded variable space \(\xi \in \{-1, 0, +1\}\) the fifteen runs occupy:

\[ \text{Corner points: }(\xi_1,\xi_2,\xi_3)\in\{-1,+1\}^3 \quad(8\text{ runs}) \] \[ \text{Face centres: one }\xi_i=0,\text{ others }\in\{-1,+1\} \quad(6\text{ runs}) \] \[ \text{Centre point: }(0,0,0)\quad(1\text{ run}) \]

The coded variables map to natural variables via \(\xi_i = (x_i - x_{i,0})/\Delta x_i\).

Why average τc, not feq?

The time constant is the quantity of physical interest. Averaging the frequencies first and then inverting gives a different (and incorrect) result because the inverse operation is nonlinear:

\[ \frac{1}{2\pi\,\bar f_{\text{eq}}} \;\neq\; \overline{\left(\frac{1}{2\pi\,f_{\text{eq}}}\right)} \]

Computing \(\tau_c^{(i)} = 1/(2\pi f_{\text{eq},i})\) for each run and then averaging correctly weights each operating condition equally in the time‑constant domain, which is the domain used in the plant model.

\[ \bar\tau_c = \frac{1}{15}\sum_{i=1}^{15}\frac{1}{2\pi f_{\text{eq},i}} \approx 0.0227\;\text{s} \]

Thermal softening

At higher cutting speeds the heat flux at the tool‑chip interface increases, raising the temperature of the chip. Above roughly 600 °C the flow stress of steel decreases, reducing the shear force required to form the chip. The net effect is that \(F_c\) decreases with \(V_c\), captured in the Step 2 model by a negative exponent on \(V_c\).

\[ \tau_s(T) \approx \tau_0 \left(1 - \frac{T - T_0}{T_m - T_0}\right)^\alpha \]

where \(\tau_0\) is the flow stress at reference temperature \(T_0\), \(T_m\) is the melting point, and \(\alpha\) is a material‑specific softening exponent. The net effect is that \(F_c\) decreases with \(V_c\), captured in the model by a negative exponent on \(V_c\).
