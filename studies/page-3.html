Chapters 4 – 7  ·  PSSMC Mathematics

The Full
Control Law

Four layers, one controller. The equivalent control \(u_{\text{eq}}\) keeps the system on the sliding surface; the switching law \(u_{\text{sw}}\) restores it when disturbances push it away; the predictive extension anticipates delay; and the adaptive laws self-tune all three gains in real time.

4.1  Structure of the Control Law — Eq. (16–17)

The PSSMC output \(u(t)\) is the sum of two additive components, each with a distinct physical role. The controller output augments the nominal cutting force:

Control law structure — Equation (16) \[ u(t) = u_{\text{eq}}(t) + u_{\text{sw}}(t) \]
Augmented cutting force — Equation (17) \[ F_c(t) = F_{\text{cut}}(t) + u(t) \]

\(u_{\text{eq}}\) — Equivalent Control

The nominal component. Computed by enforcing \(\dot{s} = 0\) — it provides exactly the force needed to keep the system on the sliding surface \(s = 0\) in the absence of disturbances. Think of it as a precise model-based feedforward.

\(u_{\text{sw}}\) — Switching Control

The corrective component. Activated whenever \(s \neq 0\) — it drives the state back to the surface with force \(K\) in the direction of \(-\text{sign}(s)\). Robust to disturbances and model uncertainty by design.

4.2  Equivalent Control Derivation — Eq. (18–22)

The equivalent control is derived by enforcing the surface-invariance condition: if the system is already on the surface \(s = 0\), what control input is required to keep it there? The answer comes from setting \(\dot{s}(t) = 0\) and solving for \(u\).

Physical interpretation of each term in \(u_{\text{eq}}\):
\(k_1 x_1\) — cancels the elastic restoring force of the tool spring  |  \(c_1 x_2\) — compensates tool structural damping  |  \(-m_1 x_2\) — injects virtual damping at rate \(\lambda\)  |  \(-F_{\text{cut}}\) — cancels the current cutting force to prevent it driving the system off the surface.

4.3  Switching Control — From Sign to Tanh — Eq. (23–24)

The classical switching law uses the discontinuous sign function. It is maximally aggressive — switching instantaneously between \(+K\) and \(-K\) as \(s\) crosses zero. Near \(s = 0\), measurement noise triggers rapid switching — the well-known chattering problem — which excites mechanical resonances and degrades surface finish.

Classical switching — Eq. (23) \[ u_{\text{sw}}^{\text{classic}}(t) = K\,\text{sgn}\bigl(s(t)\bigr) \]

The PSSMC replaces the sign function with the hyperbolic tangent, producing a continuous, smooth switching control that eliminates chattering:

Smoothed switching — Eq. (24) \[ u_{\text{sw}}(t) = K\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t)}{\phi}\right) \]

where \(\phi > 0\) is the boundary layer thickness. For \(|s| \gg \phi\), \(\tanh(s/\phi) \to \pm 1\) — full restoring force, identical to \(\text{sgn}(s)\). As \(s \to 0\), \(\tanh\) decreases smoothly to zero — eliminating chattering entirely.

4.4  Complete Standard SMSC Law — Eq. (25)

Assembling the equivalent control (22) and the smoothed switching control (24) gives the Smoothed SMC (SMSC) baseline — the foundation from which the predictive and adaptive extensions are built:

Standard SMSC — Equation (25) \[ u(t) = k_1 x_1 + (c_1 - m_1)\,x_2 - F_{\text{cut}} + K\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t)}{\phi}\right) \]
This is the SMSC baseline — a single, computable expression using only current state measurements \(x_1, x_2\) and the cutting force model. The tanh term delivers full robustness while remaining infinitely differentiable. All subsequent extensions modify only the arguments of tanh and the values of \(\phi\) and \(K\).

5.1  The Problem of Actuator Delay

Any physical actuator introduces delay. In a CNC machine, the servo drive contributes \(\tau_s \approx 0.17\;\text{s}\) and the cutting process contributes \(\tau_c \approx 0.0227\;\text{s}\) — a total of nearly 0.2 seconds. A controller acting on the current \(s(t)\) issues a command that takes effect well after chatter may have grown substantially. The predictive extension transforms the controller from reactive to proactive.

5.2  First-Order Taylor Prediction — Eq. (26)

Predicted surface — Equation (26) \[ \hat{s}(t + T_p) \approx s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\,T_p \]

where \(T_p > 0\) is the prediction horizon. This is a trend-based prediction: the controller observes where the surface is and how fast it is moving, then extrapolates to estimate its future position. The rate \(\dot{s}(t)\) is computed from Eq. (15) of Page 2 using the current state — no differential equations need to be integrated online.

5.3  Modified Switching Law — Eq. (27)

Predictive switching — Equation (27) \[ u_{\text{sw}}(t) = K\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{\hat{s}(t + T_p)}{\phi}\right) = K\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\,T_p}{\phi}\right) \]
This is the PSMC switching law. The controller issues its corrective action \(T_p\) seconds before the disturbance peaks — the command arrives at the plant at precisely the moment it is needed. All computation uses only quantities already available at the current time step.

6.1  Motivation for Adaptation

Standard SMC uses fixed gains \(\lambda\), \(T_p\), and \(\phi\). A fixed \(\lambda\) forces a compromise: large enough for worst-case chatter, it is unnecessarily aggressive during calm cutting — wasting energy, increasing noise sensitivity, and shortening tool life. The PSSMC resolves this by making all three parameters continuously adaptive — they self-tune in real time based on the instantaneous surface value \(s(t)\).

6.2  Adaptive Law for \(\lambda\) — Eq. (28)

Adaptive sliding gain — Equation (28) \[ \dot{\lambda}(t) = -\alpha_1\,s(t)^2, \qquad \lambda \geq \lambda_{\min} = 1.0\;\text{s}^{-1} \]

When \(|s|\) is large (heavy chatter), \(s^2\) is large and \(\lambda\) decreases rapidly toward its minimum. When \(s \to 0\) (calm cutting), \(\dot{\lambda} \to 0\) — \(\lambda\) freezes at the value it has learned. The lower bound \(\lambda_{\min} = 1.0\;\text{s}^{-1}\) prevents singularity.

6.3  Adaptive Law for \(T_p\) — Eq. (29)

Adaptive prediction horizon — Equation (29) \[ \dot{T}_p(t) = -\alpha_2\,s(t)^2, \qquad T_p \geq T_{p,\min} = 10\;\mu\text{s} \]

The prediction horizon adapts with the same \(s^2\) signal — long when the system is evolving rapidly, short when calm. The lower bound \(T_{p,\min} = 10\;\mu\text{s}\) prevents zero-horizon degeneration.

6.4  Adaptive Law for \(\phi\) — Eq. (30)

Adaptive boundary layer — Equation (30) \[ \dot{\phi}(t) = -\alpha_3\,|s(t)|, \qquad \phi \geq \phi_{\min} = 10^{-4} \]

The \(|s|\) driver (rather than \(s^2\)) produces a slower, more gradual adaptation of the boundary layer — appropriate because aggressive changes to \(\phi\) can destabilise the switching law. The lower bound \(\phi_{\min} = 10^{-4}\) is enforced.

The unifying logic: all three parameters are driven by \(s(t)\). When the system is in distress (large \(|s|\)), all three adapt rapidly. When the system converges (small \(|s|\)), all three freeze — the controller has learned the appropriate gains for the current operating condition. The PSSMC is simultaneously aggressive when the machine demands it and gentle when it does not.

7.  Complete PSSMC Control Law — Eq. (31–32)

Assembling the equivalent control (22), the predictive switching law (27), and the three adaptive laws (28–30) gives the full PSSMC:

Complete PSSMC — Equations (31–32)
Control output — Eq. (31) \[ u(t) = k_1 x_1 + \bigl(c_1 - \lambda(t)\,m_1\bigr)\,x_2 - F_{\text{cut}} + K\,\tanh\!\left(\frac{s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\,T_p(t)}{\phi(t)}\right) \]
Parameter dynamics — Eq. (32) \[ \dot{\lambda} = -\alpha_1 s^2, \qquad \dot{T}_p = -\alpha_2 s^2, \qquad \dot{\phi} = -\alpha_3 |s| \]

The total system state is \(\mathbf{x}_{\text{ext}} = [x_1,\, x_2,\, x_3,\, x_4,\, \lambda,\, T_p,\, \phi]^T\) — a seven-dimensional system whose dynamics are fully specified by Equations (5)–(8) and (31)–(32). At every sampling instant, the controller computes \(u(t)\) through a fixed sequence of algebraic operations — no matrix inversions, no iterative solvers, no online optimisation.

Control structure overview

The PSSMC is a cascade of four ideas, each solving a specific weakness of the previous:

SMC → robust to disturbances, but chatters near \(s = 0\).
+ tanh (SMSC) → eliminates chattering, but still reactive to current \(s\).
+ Taylor prediction (PSMC) → anticipates where \(s\) will be after actuator delay.
+ adaptation (PSSMC) → self-tunes all gains for current operating conditions.

Each layer adds one equation and one parameter. The full PSSMC adds only three extra ODEs to the standard SMSC — a minimal price for full adaptivity.

The chattering problem

The sign function \(\text{sgn}(s)\) switches between \(+K\) and \(-K\) instantaneously as \(s\) passes through zero. In a real system with measurement noise and finite sampling rates, \(s\) never stays exactly at zero — it oscillates around it at high frequency.

Each sign switch fires the actuator at full gain in alternating directions — a rapid, high-energy oscillation called chattering. This excites mechanical resonances, causes premature actuator wear, and ironically worsens the surface finish the controller is trying to protect.

Replacing \(\text{sgn}(s)\) with \(\tanh(s/\phi)\) eliminates chattering because \(\tanh\) is continuous and differentiable — it never switches discontinuously. Near \(s = 0\), the output is proportional to \(s/\phi\), delivering gentle corrective forces rather than bang-bang switching.

Why predict the surface?

Consider a chatter event starting at \(t = 0\). The sensor measures \(s(0)\), the controller computes \(u(0)\), and the actuator applies the force at \(t \approx 0.19\;\text{s}\) after both delays.

Without prediction: by \(t = 0.19\;\text{s}\), chatter amplitude may have doubled or tripled. The corrective force is too late and too small.

With prediction: at \(t = 0\), the controller computes \(\hat{s}(0.19) \approx s(0) + \dot{s}(0)\times 0.19\) and issues the force needed to counteract the future chatter peak. The command arrives precisely when needed — proactive suppression.

Why adapt the parameters?

The 15 experimental runs in Chapter 1 showed that \(\tau_c\) varies from 0.0075 s to 0.071 s — a ten-fold range. The optimal \(\lambda = 1/\tau_c\) therefore ranges from 14.1 to 133.3 s⁻¹.

A fixed \(\lambda\) can only be optimal at one operating point. At all other conditions it is either too aggressive (noise amplification, wear) or too sluggish (slow convergence, residual chatter). Fixed \(T_p\) and \(\phi\) have the same problem.

Adaptation solves this: the controller continuously measures \(|s|\) — a direct indicator of how much chatter is present — and adjusts all three parameters in real time. No offline re-tuning, no look-up tables, no operating condition detection logic.
