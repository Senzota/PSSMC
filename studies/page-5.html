Chapter 10  ·  PSSMC Mathematics

Results & Discussion

The proof in Chapter 8 guarantees the PSSMC must work. This chapter shows — through numerical simulation of the Kasprowiak two-DOF model with zero damping — how well it actually does. Three controllers are placed side-by-side: no control, classical SMC, and the PSSMC. The verdict is quantitative.

What the numbers mean. No control produces clean undamped chatter at \(\approx 70\,\mu\text{m}\) RMS — a destructive resonance. Classical SMC actually worsens RMS (\(\approx 79\,\mu\text{m}\)) because aggressive switching injects high-frequency noise even while suppressing the dominant mode. 5.2  Tool-Tip Displacement \(y_1(t)\)

\(y_1(t)\) is the displacement of the tool tip — the physical quantity responsible for surface finish on the workpiece. Three traces are overlaid on the same axes for the first 10 ms after engagement.

The red trace (no control) shows the characteristic regenerative-chatter envelope — an oscillation that does not decay because \(c_1 = c_2 = 0\). The amber trace (SMC) reaches a similar amplitude but with a visibly rougher, higher-frequency texture: that texture is the chatter of the controller itself. The cyan trace (PSSMC) is the smoothest and the smallest — the adaptive boundary layer \(\phi(t)\) softens the switching.

5.3  Cutting Force \(F_{cut}(t)\)

\(F_{cut}(t)\) is the regenerative force at the cutting edge, computed from the chip-thickness variation \(K_c \cdot a_p \cdot (f_z - (y_1 - y_1^{\text{prev}}))\). It is the disturbance the controller must reject.

5.4  Control Signal \(U(t)\) — and its Decomposition

The control signal \(U(t) = U_{eq}(t) + U_{sw}(t)\) splits into two physically distinct components: \(U_{eq}\) — the smooth equivalent control that keeps the system on the sliding surface — and \(U_{sw}\) — the switching part that rejects disturbances. SMC uses a constant \(K_{sw}\); PSSMC modulates \(\phi(t)\) to soften the switch.

5.8  Discussion

The three traces tell a single story in three voices.

Why SMC worsens RMS

Classical SMC uses a hard \(\mathrm{sign}(s)\) switch. With \(c_1=0\), there is no natural damping to absorb the high-frequency content of that switch, so the controller injects chatter that the plant cannot dissipate. The result: lower bias but higher RMS.

How PSSMC wins

PSSMC replaces \(\mathrm{sign}\) with \(\tanh(s/\phi)\) and lets \(\phi(t)\) grow when chatter rises. The control becomes locally smooth exactly where smoothness is needed — a soft boundary layer that breathes with the operating point.

Why the energy halves

Adaptive \(\lambda(t)\) tightens the sliding surface only when error grows. Most of the time \(\lambda(t)\) sits near its lower bound, so the controller acts gently. Standard SMC keeps a fixed \(\lambda\) that is conservative for the worst case and thus over-actuates on average.

What no-damping means

\(c_1 = c_2 = 0\) is the hardest case for any controller: any energy injected stays in the system. That the PSSMC still cuts RMS by ~50% under this regime is strong evidence the same gains will perform even better on a real, slightly damped machine.

The role of the Lyapunov proof

The simulation passes; the proof on Page 4 says it must pass — for any bounded disturbance, any initial condition. The numbers here are confirmation, not validation. The validation was already algebraic.

What's left to do

Hardware implementation, multi-axis extension, machine-learning gain tuning, and head-to-head against fractional-order SMC and model-predictive control. See Page 6 — Future Works for the road ahead.

Simulation method

The Kasprowiak two-DOF chatter model is integrated with SciPy's Radau solver (implicit Runge–Kutta, stiff-friendly) over \(t \in [0,\ 10\ \text{ms}]\) at 2000 internal time points, downsampled to 1000 for display.

The same model, the same disturbance profile, and the same initial conditions are used for all three controllers. Only the control law \(U(t)\) changes. This makes the comparison fully controlled — any difference in \(y_1(t)\) is attributable to the controller alone.

System parameters: \(m_1 = 0.2\ \text{kg}\), \(m_2 = 3.0\ \text{kg}\), \(k_1 = 2.2 \times 10^9\ \text{N/m}\), \(k_2 = 1.7 \times 10^8\ \text{N/m}\), \(c_1 = c_2 = 0\), \(K_c = 1500\ \text{N/mm}^2\), \(a_p = 0.5\ \text{mm}\), \(f_z = 0.2\ \text{mm}\).

Why no damping?

Setting \(c_1 = c_2 = 0\) is the worst case. Real CNC machines have small but non-zero structural damping. Any energy a controller injects must be removed by the controller itself — the machine will not help.

This choice is deliberate. A controller that handles \(c = 0\) will handle every realistic case strictly better. It is the harshest possible benchmark, and the PSSMC still wins.

Mathematically, with \(c = 0\) the plant has a pair of purely imaginary eigenvalues at \(\pm j\omega_n\). Any disturbance excites pure undamped oscillation. The controller is the only source of dissipation.

How metrics are computed

RMS \(y_1\): \(\sqrt{\tfrac{1}{T}\int_0^T y_1^2(t)\,dt\}\), evaluated over the full 10 ms window with the trapezoidal rule.

Chatter reduction: \((\mathrm{RMS}_{\text{SMC}} - \mathrm{RMS}_{\text{PS}}) / \mathrm{RMS}_{\text{SMC}} \times 100\%\). Negative would mean PSSMC was worse.

Energy reduction: Define control energy \(E = \int_0^T U^2(t)\,dt\). Then \((E_{\text{SMC}} - E_{\text{PS}}) / E_{\text{SMC}} \times 100\%\). This measures total actuator effort — directly tied to wear, heat, and electrical consumption on a real machine.

Reading the tool-tip displacement \(y_1(t)\)

Three traces share the same time axis. Each shows the tool-tip displacement (in micrometres) under a different controller. The flatter and slimmer the trace, the better the chatter is being suppressed.

The red trace — No Control

A pure sinusoid at the dominant chatter frequency. Because \(c_1 = c_2 = 0\), nothing dissipates the oscillation — it neither grows nor shrinks, just persists at \(\pm 100\,\mu\text{m}\). This is the destructive reference.

The amber trace — Standard SMC

You can see a roughly similar envelope to the red — but with a visibly rougher, sawtoothed texture. That texture is the controller's own high-frequency switching bleeding through. SMC suppresses the mean displacement but adds chatter of its own.

The cyan trace — PSSMC

Smooth, low-amplitude, well below the red. The boundary layer \(\phi(t)\) suppresses the high-frequency switching that contaminated SMC, while adaptive \(\lambda(t)\) keeps the surface tight without over-actuating.

One-line takeaway: the cyan band's vertical thickness, divided by the red band's vertical thickness, is roughly the chatter-reduction ratio.

Reading the cutting force \(F_{cut}(t)\)

\(F_{cut}\) is the regenerative force at the tool–workpiece interface: \(F_{cut}(t) = K_c\, a_p\, [f_z - (y_1(t) - y_1(t - \tau))]\). It is the disturbance the controller has to fight.

Red — No Control

A clean periodic force at the chatter frequency. The full amplitude of the regenerative term is felt by the machine — this is what damages the tool and the surface.

Amber — SMC

Slightly noisier and slightly bigger than the red trace. Because SMC's chatter actually moves the tool more, the regenerative term gets larger.

Cyan — PSSMC

Visibly smaller envelope. Less tool motion means less chip-thickness variation, which means a smaller \(F_{cut}\) — a virtuous cycle.

Reading the control signal \(U(t)\)

\(U(t)\) is what the actuator is asked to produce. Two controllers' outputs are overlaid. The vertical extent and the texture together tell you the cost.

Amber — \(U_{\text{SMC}}(t)\)

A jagged, full-swing signal. \(U_{sw} = K_{sw}\,\mathrm{sign}(s)\) hits ±500 N constantly. The actuator is being driven hard at high frequency — wear, heat, and electrical noise are the price.

Cyan — \(U_{\text{PSSMC}}(t)\)

Far smaller envelope and much smoother. The \(\tanh\) boundary layer keeps \(U_{sw}\) bounded only by what is actually needed, and adaptive \(\lambda\) keeps \(U_{eq}\) modest.

This is where the 58% energy saving lives. Squaring the difference in vertical extent and integrating is the energy ratio.

Reading the equivalent control \(U_{eq}(t)\)

\(U_{eq}\) is the model-based feed-forward component that would keep the system exactly on the sliding surface if the model were perfect and there were no disturbance.

Amber — SMC equivalent control

Tracks the system dynamics smoothly. No chatter here — \(U_{eq}\) by construction is continuous. Its magnitude reflects how aggressive the fixed \(\lambda\) is.

Cyan — PSSMC equivalent control

Quieter and smaller. Because \(\lambda(t)\) starts at its lower bound and only rises when error builds, \(U_{eq}\) does less work most of the time.

Key insight

\(U_{eq}\) is where the intelligence lives. It is what the controller would output if there were no chatter to suppress. Comparing it across SMC and PSSMC tells you whether the adaptive law is being efficient.

Reading the switching control \(U_{sw}(t)\) — where chatter lives

\(U_{sw}\) is the discontinuous part of the controller. In SMC it is \(K_{sw}\,\mathrm{sign}(s)\); in PSSMC it is \(K_{sw}\,\tanh(s/\phi(t))\). This is the single most important chart for understanding chatter.

Amber — \(U_{sw}^{\text{SMC}}(t) = K_{sw}\,\mathrm{sign}(s)\)

A square wave between \(+500\) and \(-500\). Every time \(s\) crosses zero, the signal flips instantly. With no damping, the plant cannot filter this — it propagates straight through to \(y_1\). This square wave is the chatter.

Cyan — \(U_{sw}^{\text{PSSMC}}(t) = K_{sw}\,\tanh(s/\phi(t))\)

Smooth transitions. When \(s\) is small, \(\tanh(s/\phi)\) is small — the controller does not slam to ±\(K_{sw}\). The adaptive \(\phi(t)\) widens whenever \(s\) gets noisy, automatically softening the boundary.

Why this single chart explains everything: the difference between the amber square wave and the cyan smooth curve is the chattering. Eliminate the square wave, eliminate chatter. Eliminate chatter, halve the RMS. That is the entire result, in one picture.

Reading the sliding surface \(s(t)\)

\(s(t) = \dot e(t) + \lambda(t)\, e(t)\) is the geometric quantity the controller drives to zero. When \(s = 0\), the error is decaying exponentially with rate \(\lambda\). A good controller keeps \(s\) close to zero, ideally hugging it.

Amber — \(s_{\text{SMC}}(t)\)

Oscillates around zero with visible chatter. SMC keeps \(|s|\) small but the rapid switching of \(U_{sw}\) makes the surface itself jitter.

Cyan — \(s_{\text{PSSMC}}(t)\)

Smaller, smoother. The adaptive \(\phi(t)\) allows the controller to relax inside the boundary layer, and \(\lambda(t)\) only tightens when needed. The result is a surface that genuinely sticks near zero.

What 'hugging zero' looks like

Reaching the surface (\(s=0\)) is called the reaching phase; staying there is the sliding phase. PSSMC spends almost the entire window in the sliding phase.

Reading the adaptive parameters \(\lambda(t)\), \(T_p(t)\), \(\phi(t)\)

PSSMC's three adaptive gains evolve in real time according to the laws derived in Chapter 7. Each parameter trades smoothness against tracking. The chart shows them on three sub-axes, all on the same time base.

\(\lambda(t)\) — sliding-surface slope

Rises when \(|s|\) grows (the surface needs to be tightened) and decays back toward its lower bound otherwise. Starts at 44.1 and modulates throughout the window.

\(T_p(t)\) — prediction horizon

Increases when error is small (look further ahead) and shrinks when error grows (focus on now). Sits between \(10^{-6}\) and \(5\times10^{-4}\) s.

\(\phi(t)\) — boundary layer

Widens when the controller starts chattering (the \(|s|\) is small but oscillating) and tightens when the system is calm. This is the parameter that makes the difference vs SMC.

The trick: all three move independently, each driven by its own gradient law. The controller adapts in three directions simultaneously — that is what 'PS-SMC' (Predictive Sliding-mode SMC) means.

Reading the phase portrait

Tracking-error velocity \(\dot e\) on the vertical axis, tracking error \(e\) on the horizontal. A controller that drives \(s = \dot e + \lambda e \to 0\) collapses the trajectory onto the line \(\dot e = -\lambda\,e\).

Red cloud — No Control

A large closed loop, far from the sliding line. No collapse — the system orbits freely.

Amber band — SMC

A noisy band roughly along the sliding line. The thickness of the band is the chatter — the trajectory is on the line on average but constantly jittering off it.

Cyan ribbon — PSSMC

A slim ribbon tightly hugging the sliding line. Thickness is dramatically smaller. This is the visual signature of practical stability at work.

The diagonal reference

A dashed grey line shows \(\dot e = -\lambda_0\, e\) — the target. The closer the trajectory hugs that line, the better the controller is.
