Control Objective
& Sliding Surface

The PSSMC suppresses chatter by driving tool displacement \(y_1(t)\) to zero. A precisely designed sliding surface converts that goal into a scalar signal \(s(t)\) whose sign and magnitude guide every action the controller takes.

2.1  Control Objective

The PSSMC targets tool displacement \(y_1(t)\) as the controlled variable. When \(y_1 \to 0\), the regenerative chatter loop loses its driving signal — the surface waviness left by the previous cut vanishes and the feedback mechanism that sustains chatter collapses. The desired reference is therefore the zero equilibrium:

Reference — Equation (R) \[ y_{\text{ref}}(t) = 0 \]

2.2  Tracking Error — Eq. (9)

The tracking error is the signed deviation of the tool from its desired zero position. Using the state vector \(\mathbf{x} = [x_1,\, x_2,\, x_3,\, x_4]^T = [y_1,\, \dot{y}_1,\, y_2,\, \dot{y}_2]^T\):

Tracking error — Equation (9) \[ e(t) = 0 - y_1(t) = -x_1(t) \]

Differentiating with respect to time — noting that \(\dot{y}_{\text{ref}} = 0\):

Error rate — Equation (10) \[ \dot{e}(t) = -\dot{y}_1(t) = -x_2(t) \]
Key identity: the error rate equals the negative of tool velocity. This relationship — \(e = -x_1\), \(\dot{e} = -x_2\) — propagates through every derivation that follows, from the sliding surface to the Lyapunov proof.

3.1  The Sliding Surface — Eq. (11)

Rather than driving \(e(t)\) to zero directly, SMC defines a surface in error space and drives the system onto it first. Once on the surface, the error decays to zero automatically.

Sliding surface — Equation (11) \[ s(t) = \dot{e}(t) + \lambda\, e(t) \]

where \(\lambda > 0\) is the sliding gain. Substituting \(e = -x_1\) and \(\dot{e} = -x_2\):

Surface in state variables — Equation (12) \[ s(t) = -x_2(t) - \lambda\, x_1(t) \]
Physical interpretation: \(s(t)\) is a weighted combination of tool velocity and position. When \(s = 0\), the tool is decelerating at exactly the rate needed to reach zero with exponential decay — neither too fast (overshoot) nor too slow (sluggish response).

3.2  Why This Surface Guarantees Convergence

When \(s(t) = 0\) for all \(t \geq t_0\), Equation (11) becomes a first-order ODE in \(e(t)\):

On-surface ODE — Equation (13) \[ \dot{e}(t) + \lambda\, e(t) = 0 \]

This has the exponential solution:

Exponential decay — Equation (14) \[ e(t) = e(t_0)\,e^{-\lambda(t-t_0)} \]
The fundamental power of SMC: the error decays at rate \(\lambda\) — a geometric property of the surface itself, independent of the plant dynamics or external disturbances. Once on the surface, stability is guaranteed regardless of parameter variations or bounded chatter forces.

Sign of \(s(t)\) — Geometric Interpretation

+

\(s(t) > 0\) — Above surface

Tool displacement is too large and growing faster than \(\lambda\) can correct. Controller pushes downward toward \(s = 0\). Chatter is intensifying.

−

\(s(t) < 0\) — Below surface

Tool is recovering too quickly — overshoot risk. Controller pushes upward to prevent the system from crossing \(s = 0\) with excess momentum.

0

\(s(t) = 0\) — On surface

Error is decaying exponentially at rate \(\lambda\). The controller's entire task: keep \(s(t) = 0\) at all times through the control law.

Phase Plane — \(e\) vs \(\dot{e}\)

The sliding surface \(s = \dot{e} + \lambda e = 0\) is a straight line through the origin with slope \(-\lambda\). Any trajectory that reaches the line follows it to the origin at rate \(e^{-\lambda t}\). Adjust \(\lambda\) with the slider above to see the surface rotate.

3.3  Time Derivative of \(s(t)\) — Eq. (15)

The derivative \(\dot{s}(t)\) is required by both the predictive extension (Ch. 5) and the Lyapunov stability proof (Ch. 8). Differentiating Eq. (11) and substituting the tool dynamics from Eq. (6):

Surface derivative — Equation (15) \[ \dot{s}(t) = \frac{k_1 x_1 + c_1 x_2 - F_{\text{cut}}}{m_1} - x_2 \]
Computable in real time: all terms in Eq. (15) — \(x_1\), \(x_2\), and \(F_{\text{cut}}\) — are directly measurable state variables. No numerical differentiation is required, and the computation per sample is a fixed number of arithmetic operations. This expression feeds directly into the Taylor prediction \(\hat{s}(t+T_p) \approx s(t) + \dot{s}(t)\,T_p\) in Page 3.

Physical Bounds on \(\lambda\)

Since \(\lambda\) is the inverse of the cutting-process time constant \(\tau_c\), its bounds are derived directly from the 15 experimental runs of Chapter 1:

\[ \lambda_{\min} = \frac{1}{\tau_{c,\max}} = \frac{1}{0.0710} = 14.1\;\text{s}^{-1} \qquad \lambda_{\max} = \frac{1}{\tau_{c,\min}} = \frac{1}{0.0075} = 133.3\;\text{s}^{-1} \]

The nominal value \(\lambda_0 = 1/\bar{\tau}_c = 1/0.0227 = 44.1\;\text{s}^{-1}\) is used as the starting point for the adaptive law derived in Chapter 6. The ten-fold range across operating conditions is precisely why \(\lambda\) must be adaptive — a fixed gain cannot serve all 15 experimental conditions simultaneously.

Handover to Page 3: the surface \(s(t) = \dot{e} + \lambda e\), its derivative \(\dot{s}(t)\), and the identities \(e = -x_1\), \(\dot{e} = -x_2\) are passed to the full control law. The equivalent control \(u_{\text{eq}}\) is derived by enforcing \(\dot{s} = 0\), and the switching control \(u_{\text{sw}}\) restores the system to the surface when chatter forces push it away.

Why \(y_1\), not cutting force?

Cutting force \(F_c\) is an output of the chatter dynamics — it results from vibration rather than causing it directly. Controlling \(F_c\) to a setpoint does not prevent the regenerative mechanism from operating.

Tool displacement \(y_1\), by contrast, is the direct physical quantity in the regenerative loop. When \(y_1 \to 0\), the surface waviness deposited by the tool disappears, and there is no regenerative disturbance to sustain chatter on the next pass.

This choice also simplifies the error definition: the reference is simply \(y_{\text{ref}} = 0\), so all reference derivatives vanish from every derivation.

The Sliding Mode Control idea

Classical control drives the error directly to zero. SMC takes a different route: it first defines a surface \(s(t) = 0\) in the error space, then splits the control problem into two phases:

Reaching phase: drive the system state from wherever it starts onto the sliding surface \(s = 0\).

Sliding phase: keep the state on the surface. Once there, the closed-loop dynamics are governed entirely by the surface equation — independent of plant uncertainties or bounded disturbances.

This separation is the core power of SMC: robustness is built into the surface geometry, not into the gain tuning.

Physical meaning of \(\lambda\)

\(\lambda\) is the convergence rate of the closed-loop error on the sliding surface. Once \(s = 0\), the error satisfies \(e(t) = e_0 e^{-\lambda t}\), so:

\[ t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda} \]

For \(\lambda = 44.1\;\text{s}^{-1}\) (nominal), the error halves every \(\approx 16\;\text{ms}\). In 100 ms the error is at 1.2% of its initial value.

Larger \(\lambda\) means faster decay but also more aggressive control action and higher sensitivity to measurement noise — the adaptive law in Chapter 6 balances this automatically.

Deriving \(\dot{s}(t)\) — Step by Step

1
Differentiate the surface definition \(\dot{s}(t) = \ddot{e}(t) + \lambda\dot{e}(t)\)
2
Since \(e = -x_1\), then \(\ddot{e} = -\dot{x}_2\) From tool dynamics Eq. (6): \(\dot{x}_2 = \frac{-k_1 x_1 - c_1 x_2 + F_{\text{cut}}}{m_1}\)
3
And \(\lambda\dot{e} = -\lambda x_2\) Combining: \(\dot{s} = \frac{k_1 x_1 + c_1 x_2 - F_{\text{cut}}}{m_1} - x_2\)
4
All terms measurable — no differentiation needed \(x_1, x_2\) from state sensors; \(F_{\text{cut}}\) from force model or dynamometer.
